# Voice architecture (CEM3396)

The audio path of the six voices, and how the firmware reaches it. This pairs
with the firmware [voice engine](../firmware/voice-engine.md) (#8); the CV
delivery mechanism is in [dac-cv-mux.md](dac-cv-mux.md). The schematic (Bob
Grieb's redrawn set on untergeek; `bit-hack/OberheimMatrix1000`) is the authority
for the analog wiring — this doc focuses on what the firmware drives.

## One chip per voice

Each of the six voices is a single **Curtis CEM3396** (24-pin DIP) — a complete
analog voice on a chip, designed for exactly this microprocessor-controlled,
multiplexed-CV use:

- **2 DCOs** (oscillators) with sync,
- a **waveshaper** (saw + variable-width pulse),
- a **multimode VCF** (the Matrix-1000 uses it as a 4-pole low-pass),
- **several VCAs**: two oscillator **balance VCAs** (the Osc-1/Osc-2 mix), a
  **VCF-modulation VCA** (filter-FM amount), and **two audio output VCAs — one
  linear, one exponential**. The two audio VCAs are what the patch model exposes
  as **VCA1** (linear, default velocity-scaled — param 31/92) and **VCA2**
  (exponential, the loudness envelope, default Env 2 — dest 12 / param 93), in
  series at the output. (Earlier revisions of this doc said "2 VCAs" — the chip
  actually has ~five; the Matrix just programs the two audio ones.)

The chip has a single multiplexed **CV input**: the host writes each control
voltage in turn and an external sample-and-hold array holds them. So "playing a
voice" is the firmware continuously refreshing its **11 control-voltage inputs**
(see dac-cv-mux.md).

## What sets pitch — a digital clock, not a CV

The CEM3396 datasheet settles what our firmware-only view had backwards. **The
chip has no pitch CV.** Each of its two waveform converters is driven by a
**digital timing signal**, and *"the frequency of these waveforms is equal to the
input digital timing signal (typically generated by a programmable divider),
allowing precise and stable control of pitch."* The block-diagram timing inputs
(pins 3/4 and 8/9, the `C_T` timing caps) are annotated **"FROM µP TIMER
(e.g. 8253) DETERMINES FREQUENCY."**

So **pitch is set by the four 8253 timer chips** (`$0000/$0400/$0800/$0C00`, 12
counters = one per DCO across the six voices), each acting as a **programmable
frequency divider**, reprogrammed per note. Because the oscillators are
clock-derived they are **crystal-stable and need no analog tuning**. This confirms
the timers' identity/role (timing-clocks.md) and **corrects** our earlier claim
that pitch was a DAC CV.

> **Firmware — traced and confirmed.** `FUN_8765` *is* the pitch primitive, and it
> writes the **8253 divider**, not a CV. In code it reads a 16-bit count from the
> **`$A652` note→divider table** (`LDD ,X++`, with interpolation for glide/fine
> pitch) and writes it **LSB-then-MSB to the same address** — the 8253 counter-load
> sequence — where that address is the voice's timer counter, cached per voice in
> the voice block (`+$75`, from the **`$8220` table** whose records hold the
> `$0000`-region counter/control addresses: voice 0 → `$0000`+`$0800`, voice 1 →
> `$0001`+`$0801`, voice 3 → `$0400`+`$0C00`, …). This matches the datasheet's
> "frequency = input timing signal," and it explains why a direct-address xref of
> `$0000…` saw only the boot init: the per-note writes go through that cached
> pointer. (An earlier revision over-corrected this to "VCF-frequency CV" — wrong;
> pitch was always `FUN_8765`, via the timer rather than the DAC.)

## What the firmware controls per voice — the 11 DAC control inputs

The datasheet lists **11 control inputs, all high-impedance, 0 to +5 V**, driven
by the multiplexed DAC (dac-cv-mux.md). Per voice they are: **waveshape A/B**,
**pulse-width A/B**, **wave-select**, **oscillator balance** (mix), **filter-FM
(modulation) amount**, **resonance**, **filter frequency**, and the two audio-VCA
gains (**linear** and **log/exp**). *Pitch is not among them* — it's the timer
clock above. These map onto the patch model's synthesis parameters (#16),
computed by the compiled per-voice program each tick (#8/#9).

## Voice feedback — the `$2018+` lines

The six per-voice status bytes at `$2018 + n×$300` (bit 7), polled by the
foreground (#7) and used by the run-time calibration (#12), are the hardware
feedback from each voice. Since the **oscillators are clock-locked and need no
tuning**, any comparator here is on the **analog side** (filter/VCA calibration)
or a per-voice service/timing strobe — *not* oscillator tuning. Its exact
electrical meaning (and why it decodes in the patch-ROM window address range) is
the one voice-side question still open against the schematic.

## Known failure points (maintenance)

From the community/service literature: the CEM3396s themselves rarely fail; the
usual suspects are the **CD4051/4053 multiplexers** in the CV path (dac-cv-mux.md)
— and the small hold caps at the chip's high-Z inputs — plus the patch-RAM
battery. A dead mux typically shows as one parameter stuck or one voice
misbehaving.
